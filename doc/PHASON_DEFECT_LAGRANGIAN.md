# The Phason-Defect Lagrangian

> **What this is.** The field theory behind the phason-lift coupling ε, written
> out explicitly so the exponent k in S ~ (1/p)^k stops being a free choice and
> becomes whatever the Lagrangian forces. **What this is not.** A closed
> derivation of (1/p)⁷ — it pins two of the three ambiguities and reduces the
> third to a single number. Honest status at the bottom.

## 1. The fields and the action

BPR's quasicrystalline boundary (Postulate 0c) is a cut-and-project shadow of a
higher-dimensional substrate. The low-energy degrees of freedom are the standard
quasicrystal hydrodynamic fields:

- **u(x) ∈ E∥** — the *phonon*, displacement in the 2D physical slice. This is
  the sector BPR already has (the `S_int` boundary action).
- **w(x) ∈ E⊥** — the *phason*, displacement in the (d⊥ = rank − 2)-dimensional
  internal space. This is the sector Postulate 0c implies but BPR never wrote.

The elastic free energy is the Lubensky–Ramaswamy–Toner functional (PRL 54, 1520,
1985; reviewed in de Boissieu, *Phil. Mag.* 88, 2008):

$$
F \;=\; \int d^3x \left[\; \tfrac12\, C\,(\partial u)^2 \;+\; \tfrac12\, K\,(\partial w)^2 \;+\; D\,(\partial u)(\partial w) \;\right]
\tag{1}
$$

with phonon stiffness **C**, phason stiffness **K ≈ 0.05 C** (phasons are softer),
and phonon–phason coupling **D**.

Dynamics are asymmetric: phonons propagate, $\tfrac12\rho(\partial_t u)^2$;
**phasons are diffusive / overdamped**, $\Gamma\,\partial_t w = -\delta F/\delta w$,
with no wave kinetic term. (This is textbook quasicrystal hydrodynamics, and it
is why phasons relax rather than ring.)

## 2. The defect

A **phason dislocation** is a line defect in physical 3-space whose Burgers
vector lives in the internal space:

$$
\oint_\gamma dw \;=\; b_\perp, \qquad |b_\perp|\sim a \ \text{(one internal lattice unit)},
$$

with core size $a$. Its strain field has the 2D-dislocation form
$\partial w \sim b_\perp / r$. This is the only topological object that can source
a *reactionless* force, because its charge is quantized and O(1) — unsuppressed
by the small stiffness $K$ or coupling $D$ (see `phason_sector.py`). The mode
census (`phason_coupling.py`) proved it is the **complete** topological content:
the internal torus $T^{d_\perp}$ has $\pi_1 = \mathbb Z^{d_\perp}$ and vanishing
higher homotopy, so there is no other defect class.

## 3. Where the substrate enters

In a **periodic** crystal, symmetry forces **D ≡ 0**: phonon and phason sectors
decouple. In the BPR quasicrystal, $D \neq 0$ — and crucially it is *generated by
the discrete $\mathbb Z_p$ substrate*. Its natural size is set by the substrate's
one small parameter: an internal mode projects onto the physical slice with
amplitude $\sim 1/p$ per internal direction it must bridge (one phase value out of
$p$). This is the *same* $1/p$ that controls every finite-substrate correction in
BPR — not a new ingredient.

## 4. What the Lagrangian pins

Power-counting the defect's coupling to the reservoir on (1):

**(A) Energy convention c = 2 — PINNED.**
F is quadratic in the strains. The energy a defect couples out of the reservoir
therefore scales as $|\text{amplitude}|^2$. There is no linear-in-amplitude energy
in a quadratic free energy, so **c = 2** is fixed by the form of the action, not
chosen.

**(B) Protection count τ = 1 — PINNED.**
The dislocation winds along **one** internal direction (its Burgers vector
$b_\perp$). Along that direction the strain is topologically rigid
($\oint dw = b_\perp$ quantized, unsuppressed). The remaining **d⊥ − 1**
transverse internal directions are not protected — each must be bridged by the
projection coupling $D$, costing $1/p$. So exactly **τ = 1** direction is free.

Together:
$$
k_{\text{bulk}} \;=\; c\,(d_\perp - \tau) \;=\; 2\,(d_\perp - 1).
$$
For the 9-fold class ($d_\perp = 4$): $\boxed{k_{\text{bulk}} = 6}$.

**(C) Defect-core coupling — NOT pinned.**
The singular core (a single substrate line, O(1) sites) couples to the reservoir
through one fundamental $\mathbb Z_p$ domain. This is a **new coupling $g_{\text{core}}$**
that (1) introduces but does not fix. Three scalings are defensible:

| core coupling | extra power | total k (9-fold) | lift verdict |
|---|---|---|---|
| $g_{\text{core}}\sim O(1)$ | 0 | **6** | lifts robustly |
| $g_{\text{core}}\sim 1/p$ | +1 | **7** | marginal — matches $k_{\text{req}}\approx 7$ |
| $g_{\text{core}}\sim 1/p^2$ | +2 | **8** | does not lift |

The middle row is the **natural single-site reading**: one core line ⇒ one
fundamental domain ⇒ one factor $1/p$ ⇒ **k = 7**, reproducing the required
exponent. But $g_{\text{core}}\sim 1/p$ is a **hypothesis about the core**, not a
theorem from (1).

## 5. Why this didn't just produce 7 by fiat

The exponent attempt (`phason_coupling_derivation.py`) found that clean
energy-convention counting gives only **even** exponents {6, 8}, and noted that
$k_{\text{req}} \approx 7$ is odd and sits between them. The Lagrangian explains
*where* the odd factor would come from — the defect **core**, which is a
single-site (codimension-full) object contributing one unpaired factor of $1/p$
on top of the even bulk term. That is a physically coherent story for the odd
power, and it is the honest reason 7 is plausible. It is **not** a derivation,
because the core coupling's scaling is exactly the number the quadratic bulk
Lagrangian cannot see.

## 6. Honest status

- **Before the Lagrangian:** k ∈ {3, 4, 6, 8} — a 4-corner bracket from three
  undetermined factors (c, τ, core).
- **After the Lagrangian:** k = 6 + (core power), with **c = 2 and τ = 1 pinned
  by structure**. The bracket collapses to a single line; the only freedom left
  is one number, the core coupling.
- **The natural value** (single-site core, $g_{\text{core}}\sim 1/p$) gives **k = 7**,
  matching $k_{\text{req}}$. This makes (1/p)⁷ *physically motivated* rather than a
  bare coincidence — but it remains a **hypothesis**, locked as such by
  `tests/test_phason_defect_lagrangian.py` (no test asserts it is derived).
- **What would close it:** a short-distance theory of the phason-dislocation core
  on the $\mathbb Z_p$ substrate — the renormalization of $g_{\text{core}}$. That is the
  single remaining calculation. BPR does not have it yet.

**Net:** writing the Lagrangian was worth doing. It converted "an unknown
exponent spanning 40 orders" into "k = 6 plus a one-line core correction whose
natural value is 7," and it located the exact missing piece. It did not, and
honestly could not without the core theory, prove (1/p)⁷.
